Literature Review

1. Theoretical Foundation

The F-Score framework synthesizes multiple theoretical perspectives on relational abuse and coercive control:

1.1 Lundy Bancroft's Entitlement Theory

Bancroft's groundbreaking work "Why Does He Do That?" establishes that abuse stems from belief systems of entitlement rather than emotional dysregulation.

"The abuser does not need to 'learn to manage his anger.' He needs to relinquish his sense of entitlement. Abuse grows from attitudes and values, not feelings."
- Lundy Bancroft

Key insights integrated into the F-Score:

  • Abuse is strategic control, not loss of control
  • Entitlement beliefs multiply harm potential
  • Traditional therapy often reinforces manipulative tactics

1.2 George K. Simon's Covert Aggression Framework

Simon's theory of character disturbance distinguishes between neurotic and character-disturbed personalities:

  • Neurotic individuals: Over-conscientious, seek safety
  • Character-disturbed: Under-conscientious, seek control

1.3 Sandra L. Brown's Pathological Love Relationships

Brown's research identifies how "supertraits" become vulnerabilities in pathological relationships:

"What makes her extraordinary in healthy contexts makes her a perfect target in pathological ones."
- Sandra L. Brown, M.A.

The LockMatch concept measures precision targeting of victim vulnerabilities.

1.4 Alexandra Klein's Neurological Impact Model

Klein's "Love and Terror" documents the neurological consequences of trauma bonding:

  • Left-right hemisphere processing split
  • Amygdala and hippocampus structural changes
  • Cognitive dissonance as measurable brain injury

2. Power Asymmetry Research

2.1 Intersectionality and Coercive Control

Research from Crenshaw (1989) and subsequent scholars demonstrates how intersecting identities compound vulnerability:

  • Financial dependence increases coercion effectiveness by 2.3x (Johnson, 2008)
  • Immigration status vulnerability correlates with 3.1x abuse persistence (Raj & Silverman, 2002)
  • Workplace harassment impacts are amplified by power differentials (MacKinnon, 1979)

2.2 Duluth Model Limitations

While the Duluth Power and Control Wheel remains influential, FIA's framework addresses its limitations:

  • Overemphasis on physical violence
  • Undervaluation of psychological manipulation
  • Binary victim-perpetrator model

3. Manipulation and Cognitive Domination

3.1 Gaslighting Research

Stern (2018) and Sweet (2019) document gaslighting's epistemic consequences:

  • Reality distortion techniques
  • Memory manipulation patterns
  • Systematic undermining of perception

3.2 Trauma Bonding Mechanisms

Carnes (1997) and subsequent researchers identify neurochemical addiction patterns:

  • Intermittent reinforcement creates strongest bonds
  • Dopamine-cortisol cycles mirror substance addiction
  • Withdrawal symptoms prevent leaving

4. Empirical Validation Studies

4.2 Cross-Cultural Validity

Testing across diverse populations reveals:

3. Mathematical Framework

Core Formula Derivation

Base harm potential:
H₀ = Σ(devaluing behaviors)

Power-adjusted harm:
H₁ = H₀ × (1 + Σ(power differentials))

Grooming-amplified harm:
F = H₁ × (1 + Σ(grooming tactics))

Total impact with nonlinear effects:
I = I_base + F + α·F²

Where α captures cascade effects at high F values
